# Phase 1 Summary

## Primary Question
What distinctive authorial voices would fill the gaps in the humanizer skill's current 12-persona registry?

## Sub-Topic Findings

### Gap Analysis
**Perspective**: none
**Researcher conclusion**: 6 of 15 cells in the 3×5 register×purpose matrix are uncovered. The gaps cluster in informal and mixed registers, with informal being the most critical: only Hemingway serves informal (narrate only), while informal explain, persuade, observe, and introspect are completely empty. Mixed explain and introspect are also empty. Domain coverage is similarly lopsided — personal/psychological has only 2 personas (both formal), technical/systems has only Le Guin. The registry also has an internal inconsistency: Thompson's persona file tags him as narrate-only, but the selection reference lists him under persuade as well. Five gaps are prioritized: Informal×Explain, Informal×Introspect, Mixed×Introspect, Formal×Narrate, and Informal×Observe.
**Relation to primary question**: Provides the exact target map — which cells need filling — against which candidate personas can be evaluated.

### Literary Voices

**Researcher conclusion**: 12 candidates identified across the prioritized gaps. Top picks by gap: **E.B. White** (informal explain — conversational clarity, "one of the most companionable voices in American prose"), **Maggie Nelson** (mixed introspect — lyric essay, fragments, theory+memoir), **Rebecca Solnit** (informal persuade — the essay as hope, unexpected connections), **Annie Ernaux** (informal introspect — flat, factual memoir, "collective autobiography"), **John McPhee** (mixed explain — patient longform explanation of anything), **Oliver Sacks** (mixed explain — clinical observation as humanism), **Annie Dillard** (informal observe — mystical attention to the natural world), **Karl Ove Knausgaard** (informal introspect — maximalist everyday consciousness), **Rachel Carson** (formal explain — scientific lyricism), **Primo Levi** (mixed explain/introspect — chemist's clarity about atrocity). Also: James Agee, Svetlana Alexievich (polyphonic documentary), Hanif Abdurraqib (music criticism as memoir).
**Relation to primary question**: Maps specific authors to specific gaps with style analyses and representative passages.

### Contemporary and Internet-Native Voices

**Relation to primary question**: Fills informal register almost completely — explain, observe, persuade, and introspect all covered by internet-native writers.

## Cross-Cutting Insights

### Convergence: The highest-priority gaps are informal explain and informal introspect
All five sub-topics independently converged on the same diagnosis: the missing informal registers are the registry's biggest weakness. The gap analysis formalized this; the literary, internet, and translated reports all produced candidates for those cells. Informal explain is the most common real-world writing scenario (blog posts, newsletters, documentation, essays) and currently has zero personas. Informal introspect is the second most critical gap — personal writing, memoir, and reflective prose are entirely unserved by the existing registry's formal-only introspective personas (Woolf, Didion).

### Overlap: Candidates appear across multiple sub-topics
Several authors were independently recommended by different research tasks: **W.G. Sebald** (literary and translated), **Maggie Nelson** (literary, implied in internet-adjacent), **Rebecca Solnit** (literary, genre-adjacent). **Annie Ernaux** and **Karl Ove Knausgaard** were both proposed for informal introspect from different angles. This independent convergence strengthens the case for these candidates — they're filling real, recognizable gaps.

### Internet-native is not a genre — it's a register
The internet-voices report found that the most distinctive contemporary voices (Lockwood, Havrilesky, Tolentino, dril) achieve effects that are inseparable from their platform context. Their styles are teachable via exemplars, but the exemplars must preserve the medium-specific texture — the newsletter's intimacy, Twitter's constraint, the blog's recursive linking. This is a different challenge from teaching canonical literary styles, but the technique (few-shot prompting with unaltered excerpts) should still work.

### Translation risk: Some voices degrade more than others
The translated-voices report identified a key concern: authors whose style is heavily dependent on sentence-level syntax (Krasznahorkai, Proust) lose more in translation than authors whose voice lives in conceptual architecture (Borges, Calvino). For the humanizer skill, this means prioritizing translated authors whose voice survives the translation layer — Borges's metaphysical concision works in English; Krasznahorkai's Hungarian sentence-as-endurance may not.

### The "too distinctive" problem
Several candidates were flagged as potentially too distinctive: **dril**'s absurdist Twitter voice might overwhelm any text it touches; **David Foster Wallace** (already in registry) is infamous for producing pastiche when imitated; **Terry Pratchett**'s footnotes-and-puns style may be hard to separate from his specific comedic content. The humanizer should be aware that the most distinctive voices are also the most likely to produce caricature — this is consistent with the research finding about "generative exaggeration" (10-20× amplification of salient traits).

### Gap in the gap analysis: No "humor" axis
The existing registry has only one comic persona (Thompson — dark/satirical). Nobody does light humor, warm comedy, or absurdism. Terry Pratchett fills this gap from genre; David Sedaris (literary voices) could fill it from memoir; dril fills it from the internet. This suggests the registry might benefit from a "tone" dimension beyond register×purpose×domain.
