# Final Report: New Authorial Personas for the Humanizer Skill

## Primary Research Question

What distinctive authorial voices would fill the gaps in the humanizer skill's current 12-persona registry?

---

## 1. Answer: Recommended Implementation Shortlist

Sixteen candidates, organized by the gap each fills. These are the authors to implement first — in priority order.

### Gap: Informal Explain (Priority 1 — the registry's largest single deficit)

Two candidates. The conversational explainer is the most commonly needed voice across all humanization use cases, and the current registry has zero personas for it.

1. **E.B. White** — The archetype. Conversational clarity, emotional restraint, Anglo-Saxon diction. His co-authorship of *The Elements of Style* means the rules governing his voice are explicit and extractable. Works: *Charlotte's Web*, *Essays of E.B. White*, "Once More to the Lake."
2. **Scott Alexander** (Astral Codex Ten / Slate Star Codex) — Epistemic transparency, steelmanning, the sense of genuine intellectual discovery on the page. Mixed-diction register that pairs "immanentize the eschaton" with "okay whatever." Highly teachable surface patterns: confidence levels, parenthetical asides, thinking-out-loud structure.

### Gap: Informal Observe (Priority 5 — casual, conversational social observation)

3. **Annie Dillard** — Mystical attention to the natural world. Fuses scientific precision with theological yearning. Observation elevated to spiritual practice. Works: *Pilgrim at Tinker Creek*.
4. **Jia Tolentino** — Clear-sighted millennial cultural criticism. Ethically serious but never preachy. Self-aware: she distrusts her own narratives while constructing them with precision. Works: *Trick Mirror*, New Yorker essays.

### Gap: Informal Introspect (Priority 2 — raw, unperformed self-examination)

5. **Heather Havrilesky** (Ask Polly) — The existential advice column as a form. Emotions maximalist: profane, intimate, all-caps sincerity. Teaches how to sit with difficult feelings. Her patterns (name the delusion → anaphoric escalation → blunt truth) are highly extractable. Works: *Ask Polly* archives (Substack / The Cut).
6. **Annie Ernaux** — Flat, factual memoir via "collective autobiography." Pronoun displacement ("we" or "she," not "I"), stripped prose, memory anchored to objects and photographs. Radically different from Woolf's flowing interiority. Works: *The Years* (trans. Alison L. Strayer).

### Gap: Informal Persuade (persuasion through intimacy, not polemic)

7. **Rebecca Solnit** — Persuasion through unexpected connections rather than polemic. Associative, digressive essays that treat hope as methodology. Rich but accessible; philosophically sophisticated but grounded in concrete example. Works: *Hope in the Dark*, *Men Explain Things to Me*.

### Gap: Mixed Introspect (Priority 3 — memoir that moves between analytical and emotional registers)

8. **Maggie Nelson** — "Autotheory": lyrical-intellectual prose that weaves Barthes, Butler, and Sedgwick through personal narrative. Short essayistic fragments, genre-refusing, fundamentally open. Works: *The Argonauts*, *Bluets*.

### Gap: Mixed Explain (longform, accessible nonfiction explanation)

9. **John McPhee** — Patient explanation of complex systems (geology, physics, transportation) rendered through wry, metaphor-rich precision. Structural intelligence that organizes vast technical material into invisible architectures. Works: *Annals of the Former World*, *Basin and Range*.

### Gap: Genre — Entirely New Domains

Three candidates from domains the registry does not cover at all: crime/noir, horror, and food writing.

10. **Raymond Chandler** — Crime/noir. The simile as moral commentary: "He looked about as inconspicuous as a tarantula on a slice of angel food." Paratactic rhythm, street vernacular fused with classical education. Mechanically teachable simile engine. Works: *The Big Sleep*, *Farewell, My Lovely*.

11. **Shirley Jackson** — Horror/domestic dread. Declarative, clean sentences that accumulate unbearable wrongness. Horror as a byproduct of precision, not excess. Teachable rhythm: normal-normal-normal-wrong. Works: *The Haunting of Hill House*, *We Have Always Lived in the Castle*.

12. **M.F.K. Fisher** — Food writing. Invented the first-person food essay. Treats appetite as metaphor for all human need. Tonal range from Hemingwayesque spareness to baroque richness. Works: *The Gastronomical Me*, *Consider the Oyster*.

### Gap: Cross-Cutting — Humor (no existing comic persona except Thompson's dark satire)

13. **Terry Pratchett** — Fantasy satire. Footnotes as comedic-thematic devices. Warmth and moral clarity disguised as comedy. Affectionate toward characters even while satirizing institutions. The only candidate who fills the light-comedy / warm-humor register that the registry entirely lacks. Works: *Small Gods*, Discworld series.

### Gap: Translation — Non-Anglo Voices (the registry is 11/12 Anglo-American)

Four candidates whose voices survive translation and bring registers English literary tradition does not naturally produce.

14. **Jorge Luis Borges** — Spanish (Argentina). Metaphysical concision: stories that read like encyclopedia entries for objects that don't exist. Erudition as poetry. Voice is remarkably stable in translation. Works: *Ficciones* (trans. Andrew Hurley), *The Aleph*.

15. **Italo Calvino** — Italian. Lightness as moral-aesthetic principle. Structural play. Modular forms (55 micro-chapters in *Invisible Cities* arranged in a thematic matrix). Warm but crystalline. Works: *Invisible Cities* (trans. William Weaver).

16. **W.G. Sebald** — German / UK. Documentary-fiction hybrid. Black-and-white photographs embedded in text. Sentences that extend across pages in melancholy accumulation. Historical trauma refracted through personal wandering. Bilingual from inception — personally supervised his English translations. Works: *Austerlitz* (trans. Anthea Bell), *The Rings of Saturn*.

## 2. Evidence Summary

### E.B. White (Informal Explain)

**Finding**: White's voice is the archetypal American informal explainer — deceptively simple, conversationally intimate, emotionally restrained. His defining technique is emotional restraint that conveys deep feeling through what's suggested rather than stated. Co-author of *The Elements of Style*, making his constraints unusually explicit: prefer the specific to the general, the concrete to the abstract, use Anglo-Saxon words over Latinate, let the detail carry the emotion.

**Source**: Literary Voices report (literary-voices_report.md). Primary sources: *Charlotte's Web* (1952), *The Elements of Style* (1959). Critical consensus triangulated across multiple literary criticism sources.

### Scott Alexander (Informal Explain)

**Finding**: Alexander writes frighteningly long, intellectually demanding essays that readers devour anyway. Signature techniques: steelmanning (representing opposing arguments better than their proponents do), epistemic transparency (prefacing posts with confidence levels), DFW-ish mixed diction, and storytelling as the vehicle for explanation. His voice is that of a brilliant friend thinking out loud, complete with asides, jokes, and the sense of witnessing genuine intellectual discovery.

**Source**: Internet Voices report (internet-voices_report.md). Primary sources: *Astral Codex Ten* (Substack), *Slate Star Codex* (archived). Secondary critical analysis by Sasha Chapin and Jason Crawford.

### Annie Dillard (Informal Observe)

**Finding**: Dillard applies mystical intensity to mundane observation, fusing scientific precision with theological yearning. Her tonal range moves from dark humor to childlike curiosity to unflinching descriptions of nature's violence. Her voice is "relentlessly first-person" yet transcends mere memoir. Teaches observation as a spiritual practice.

**Source**: Literary Voices report. Primary sources: *Pilgrim at Tinker Creek* (1974). Supported by Nieman Storyboard, Literary Hub, and Stanford Humanities Center sources.

### Jia Tolentino (Informal Observe)

**Finding**: Tolentino writes with "an inimitable mix of force lyricism and internet-honed humor" — the only writer critics identify who can incorporate meme-speak into literary prose without losing credibility. Her voice is crystalline and self-aware; she distrusts her own narratives while constructing them with precision. Compared to Joan Didion and Montaigne.

**Source**: Internet Voices report. Primary sources: *Trick Mirror* (2019), New Yorker essays. Secondary sources: NYT reviews, Slate, LARB.

### Heather Havrilesky (Informal Introspect)

**Finding**: Havrilesky invented the "existential advice column" — a form built on the premise that the person asking already knows the answer and needs permission to feel it. Her signature technique is the lyrical catalog: anaphoric sentences that build momentum toward a short, percussive revelation. Profanity signals intimacy, not shock. Teaches how to sit with difficult feelings.

**Source**: Internet Voices report. Primary sources: *Ask Polly* (The Awl → The Cut → Substack), collection *How to Be a Person in the World* (2016). Secondary: Slate review.

### Annie Ernaux (Informal Introspect)

**Finding**: Ernaux's voice is defined by "deliberate austerity" — short sentences, plain vocabulary, what the Nobel committee called "written in plain language, scraped clean." Her defining technique is pronoun displacement: "we" or "she" rather than "I," transforming personal memory into collective history. This is a radically different introspective mode from Woolf's formal interiority.

**Source**: Literary Voices report. Primary sources: *The Years* (trans. Alison L. Strayer, 2017). Nobel Prize in Literature 2022 committee citation. Supported by LARB and Paris Review critical analyses.

### Rebecca Solnit (Informal Persuade)

**Finding**: Solnit persuades through unexpected connections rather than polemic. Her defining technique is hope as methodology — even when addressing catastrophic subjects, she finds possibility without false optimism. Her prose is "hopeful but critical," a register almost entirely absent from the existing registry.

**Source**: Literary Voices report. Primary sources: *Hope in the Dark* (2004). National Book Critics Circle Award.

### Maggie Nelson (Mixed Introspect)

**Finding**: Nelson pioneered "autotheory" — melding lived experience with philosophical reflection. Her prose maintains "a feeling of and, a feeling of if, a feeling of but" — resisting resolution. Short, essayistic fragments that meander associatively then circle back. Genre-refusing and fundamentally open.

**Source**: Literary Voices report. Primary sources: *The Argonauts* (2015), *Bluets* (2009). National Book Critics Circle Award winner. Supported by LARB and publisher critical materials.

### John McPhee (Mixed Explain)

**Finding**: McPhee's voice is defined by wry, metaphor-rich precision applied to complex systems. His signature technique is the "uncanny simile" — comparisons that are "never desperate, never overreaching, yet somehow as surprising as they are precise." His structural intelligence — organizing vast technical material into invisible architectures — makes him the gold standard for explaining systems without losing narrative momentum.

**Source**: Literary Voices report. Primary sources: *Annals of the Former World* (1998), *Basin and Range* (1980). Supported by Nieman Storyboard and Literary Hub critical analyses.

### Raymond Chandler (Genre: Crime/Noir)

**Finding**: Chandler's first-person cynicism is elevated by lyrical, unexpected similes. The technique is mechanically teachable: concrete observation → unexpected comparison → slight elevation. His voice juxtaposes literary sophistication with street vernacular in a reproducible tension. "She smelled the way the Taj Mahal looked by moonlight."

**Source**: Genre Voices report. Primary sources: *The Big Sleep* (1939), *Farewell, My Lovely* (1940). Supported by multiple style analyses and *I Write Like* corpus comparisons.

### Shirley Jackson (Genre: Horror/Domestic Dread)

**Finding**: Jackson's horror is a byproduct of precision, not excess. Declarative, clean sentences accumulate an unbearable sense of wrongness. The technique is replicable: declarative descriptions of mundane details, followed by a single destabilizing word. The pattern of normal-normal-normal-wrong is a concrete teachable rhythm.

**Source**: Genre Voices report. Primary sources: *The Haunting of Hill House* (1959), *We Have Always Lived in the Castle* (1962). Supported by MDPI Humanities scholarly analysis and Literary Hub.

### M.F.K. Fisher (Genre: Food Writing)

**Finding**: Fisher invented the first-person food essay as a form. Her prose treats appetite as a metaphor for all human need — "when I write of hunger, I am really writing about love and the hunger for it." Her tonal range modulates from Hemingwayesque spareness to baroque richness depending on emotional register — a teachable modulation.

**Source**: Genre Voices report. Primary sources: *The Gastronomical Me* (1943), *Consider the Oyster* (1941). Supported by National Endowment for the Humanities.

### Terry Pratchett (Genre: Fantasy Satire / Cross-Cutting: Humor)

**Finding**: Pratchett's omniscient narrator uses footnotes as comedic-thematic devices. His voice transitions from absurdist humor to genuine philosophy — warmth and moral clarity disguised as comedy. Affectionate toward characters even while satirizing institutions. The cross-cutting insight across all reports identified humor as a missing axis in the registry; Pratchett is the only candidate who fills the light-comedy / warm-humor register.

**Source**: Genre Voices report. Primary sources: Discworld series, especially *Small Gods* (1992). Supported by multiple critical analyses.

### Jorge Luis Borges (Translation: Spanish/Argentina)

**Finding**: Borges's metaphysical concision — stories that read like encyclopedia entries for objects that don't exist — is a voice English literary tradition does not naturally produce. His translated voice is remarkably stable across translators (Hurley, di Giovanni). The "false scholarly citation" device, infinite-regress structure, and amused cosmic detachment are concrete, teachable patterns.

**Source**: Translated Voices report. Primary sources: *Ficciones* (1944, trans. Andrew Hurley). Supported by Nobel committee citations and multiple translator interviews.

**Independent convergence**: Recommended by both the Translated Voices and Literary Voices research tasks.

### Italo Calvino (Translation: Italian)

**Finding**: Calvino's "lightness as moral-aesthetic principle" treats formal constraint as a generative device. His modular structures (micro-chapters, thematic grids) provide natural templates for prompting. The *Invisible Cities* format — describe a thing through an oblique metaphorical lens — is directly replicable in few-shot. William Weaver's canonical translations are stable.

**Source**: Translated Voices report. Primary sources: *Invisible Cities* (1972, trans. William Weaver). Supported by Oulipo scholarship, Calvino's own *Six Memos for the Next Millennium*, and multiple literary reviews.

### W.G. Sebald (Translation: German / UK)

**Finding**: Sebald's documentary-fiction hybrid dissolves the wall between genres that Anglo-American tradition keeps separate. Black-and-white photographs embedded in text, sentences extending across pages in melancholy accumulation, historical trauma refracted through personal wandering. Sebald personally supervised his English translations and sometimes rewrote his own work in translation — the voice was bilingual from inception.

**Source**: Translated Voices report, with independent confirmation from the Literary Voices report. Primary sources: *Austerlitz* (2001, trans. Anthea Bell), *The Rings of Saturn* (1995, trans. Michael Hulse). Supported by The New Yorker, Gilliam Writers Group analysis, and translator interviews.

**Independent convergence**: One of three authors recommended by multiple independent sub-topics.

### Clarice Lispector (Translation: Portuguese/Brazil)

**Finding**: Lispector's mystical interiority treats the sentence as a thought-event — prose that stops, interrogates itself, reverses, dissolves. "Not even a translation can bring it to a halt." Her voice is neither confessional (like Baldwin) nor stream-of-consciousness (like Woolf) — it treats consciousness as a metaphysical event, not a psychological one. The New Directions retranslations (2011–2015) preserve her strangeness better than earlier versions.

**Source**: Translated Voices report. Primary sources: *The Passion According to G.H.* (1964, trans. Idra Novey, 2012), *Água Viva* (1973, trans. Stefan Tobler, 2012). Supported by translator Benjamin Moser's extensive scholarship and the LARB "Many Souls of Clarice Lispector's Translators" analysis.

## 3. Confidence Assessment

### High Confidence (multiple independent sources, primary sources back claims)

- **E.B. White**: His own *Elements of Style* codifies his technique. Multiple critical sources concur on his place as the archetypal informal American prose voice. The gap he fills is the single most documented deficit in the registry.

- **Scott Alexander**: Extensive public archive (10+ years of essays). Multiple independent style analyses exist (Sasha Chapin, Jason Crawford, community discussions of "the SSC voice"). Teachable patterns are directly observable in primary sources.

- **Jia Tolentino**: Book-length primary source (*Trick Mirror*) plus extensive New Yorker archive. Multiple independent reviews concur on voice characterization. MacArthur "Genius" recognition adds institutional validation.

- **Annie Ernaux**: Nobel Prize committee citation provides authoritative voice characterization. Primary text (*The Years*) directly demonstrates the techniques described. Multiple independent critical analyses agree on method.

- **Raymond Chandler**: Canonical author with 80+ years of critical analysis. The simile technique is mechanical enough to be consistently identified across independent sources. Multiple style-analysis tools and critical works concur.

- **Shirley Jackson**: Canonical author with extensive scholarly literature. The "normal-normal-normal-wrong" rhythm is identified independently by multiple critics. Primary texts directly demonstrate the technique.

- **Borges**: Stable voice across multiple translators, independently verified by translator interviews and Nobel committee citation. His concision makes voice verification from short passages possible.

- **W.G. Sebald**: Independent convergence across two sub-topics (literary and translated). Personally supervised translations ensure minimal translation degradation. Multiple critical sources agree on voice characterization.

### Medium Confidence (sources agree but are limited in number or authority)

- **Heather Havrilesky**: Primary source archive is substantial but secondary critical analysis is thinner than for canonical literary authors. The voice is genuinely distinctive but critical consensus on technique is still forming.

- **Rebecca Solnit**: Well-established author with significant critical reception, but fewer dedicated style analyses compared to White or Chandler. Voice characterization relies on book reviews rather than scholarly dissection.

- **Maggie Nelson**: Book-length primary sources and critical acclaim (NBCC), but "autotheory" as a form is recent enough that long-term critical consensus on her technique is less settled.

- **John McPhee**: Extensive critical appreciation (*Draft No. 4*, multiple Literary Hub essays) but fewer formal academic analyses of his sentence-level technique compared to Chandler or Jackson.

- **M.F.K. Fisher**: Primary sources and critical consensus on her historical importance, but fewer recent scholarly analyses of her style. NEH recognition supports claims but formal style analysis is thinner.

- **Italo Calvino**: Canonical, but fewer translator-interview sources than Borges or Sebald. The modular-structure teachability claim is well-supported by the primary texts themselves.

- **Clarice Lispector**: Extensive translator scholarship (Benjamin Moser) but fewer independent style analyses. The "voice survives translation" claim is well-supported by Moser's detailed accounts of her translation instructions, but verification requires comparing multiple translations.

- **Terry Pratchett**: Canonical and extensively analyzed, but the "too distinctive" problem flagged in the cross-cutting insights — his footnotes-and-puns style may be hard to separate from specific comedic content. Primary sources are abundant; the open question is whether few-shot prompting can reproduce the warmth without the specific Discworld scaffolding.

### Low Confidence (significant gaps, reliance on secondary sources)

- **Annie Dillard**: Highly distinctive voice with strong critical consensus, but fewer formal analyses of her teachable patterns compared to, say, White. The "mystical attention" characterization is widely agreed but harder to decompose into prompting rules.

- None of the other recommended candidates fall into this tier. Several researched but deferred candidates (Krasznahorkai, Schulz, Tawada) are low-confidence for the reasons documented in the Translated Voices report: either translation degradation makes the voice unreliable for prompting, or the voice depends on effects that require novel-length duration.

## 4. Limitations and Open Questions

### Research Coverage Gaps

**Formal × Narrate (Priority 4 gap) remains unfilled.** The gap analysis identified this as the fourth-highest-priority gap — the registry has no formal narrator, and McCarthy's mixed-register narration is too archaic for many subjects. However, the five research sub-topics did not produce a strong candidate for this cell. Authors mentioned in the gap analysis as "kind of writer" for this gap (Nabokov, Morrison, Wharton, García Márquez) were not researched in depth. This gap should be addressed in a follow-up research phase focused specifically on formal-register narrative voices.

**Romance remains unrepresented.** The Genre Voices report investigated Georgette Heyer, Nora Roberts, and Jasmine Guillory, concluding that their voices are either too narrow (Heyer's artificial period dialect), too transparent (Roberts's accessible mainstream prose), or too formula-constrained (the romance genre's structural conventions limit transferable voice distinctiveness). If romance is a necessary domain for the skill, this gap requires different candidate criteria.

**Music criticism and food writing beyond Fisher were touched but not fully explored.** Hanif Abdurraqib (Literary Voices) is a strong candidate for informal persuade/observe but was not prioritized into the top 16 due to the cap.

### Assumptions

1. **Few-shot prompting works for authorial voice.** All teachability assessments assume that the skill's existing mechanism — providing unaltered exemplar paragraphs as part of the persona prompt — successfully transfers voice characteristics. This assumption was not empirically tested within this research phase; it is inherited from the skill's existing architecture, which already does this for 12 personas.

2. **Translation quality is stable for the recommended authors.** For Borges, Calvino, Sebald, and Lispector, this is well-supported (Sebald personally supervised his translations; Borges's voice is stable across multiple translators; Calvino's Weaver translations are canonical; Lispector's New Directions retranslations are supervised by Moser). For other translated candidates not advanced to the shortlist (Krasznahorkai, Han Kang, Tawada), translation fidelity was a limiting factor in the decision.

3. **The register × purpose × domain matrix is the right taxonomy.** The gap analysis identified that axes like "tone" (especially humor) may be missing from the current framework. The cross-cutting insight about the absence of a humor axis suggests the matrix itself may benefit from revision, which is outside this report's scope.

4. **Distinctiveness vs. teachability tradeoffs were weighted correctly.** Several highly distinctive voices (dril, Sam Kriss, David Foster Wallace) were flagged as "too distinctive" — likely to produce caricature when imitated. The recommendation prioritizes distinctive-but-teachable voices over maximally distinctive ones. This tradeoff should be validated through actual prompting tests.

### Open Questions

1. **Does the "too distinctive" problem manifest at small scale?** Wallace (already in the registry) is known to produce pastiche when imitated. Pratchett, dril, and Lockwood were flagged with similar concerns. Testing 2–3 most-distinctive personas against 2–3 standard humanization tasks would provide empirical evidence on where the distinctiveness threshold lies.

2. **Do internet-native voices require platform-specific context in their exemplars?** The Internet Voices report identified that Lockwood, Havrilesky, and dril achieve effects inseparable from platform context (Twitter's constraint, the newsletter's intimacy). If few-shot exemplars must include that context to reproduce the voice, this constrains how those personas are constructed.

3. **What is the optimal number of personas?** The registry currently has 12. This report recommends adding 16, for a total of 28. Past a certain point, selection complexity will degrade the skill's usability. The gap analysis identified 5–6 genuinely empty cells; the remaining candidates fill domain gaps (genre, translation) that the matrix does not model. There is a design decision here about whether personas should fill gaps in the existing matrix, or expand the matrix itself.

4. **Are any of the recommended candidates redundant with each other?** E.B. White and Scott Alexander both fill Informal × Explain, but from very different angles (timeless clarity vs. epistemic transparency). Jia Tolentino and Annie Dillard both fill Informal × Observe, but one is a cultural critic and the other a nature mystic. Whether these distinctions matter in practice — i.e., whether users would ever choose between two personas in the same cell — has not been tested.

5. **Should some personas be purpose-constrained (like Hemingway is narrate-only)?** Most recommended personas are proposed as filling a single purpose, but several (Solnit for persuade, Nelson for introspect) could plausibly serve adjacent purposes. Deciding on purpose tags for each persona is an implementation-level decision not covered by this research.

6. **Contested translations:** Han Kang's *The Vegetarian* (Deborah Smith translation) was praised by the Man Booker International committee but Korean scholars noted embellishments from the original. Han Kang reads English and stands by the translation, which may be sufficient for the skill's purposes, but the controversy is worth noting and was a factor in Han Kang not advancing to the final shortlist.
