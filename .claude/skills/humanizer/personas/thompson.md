# Thompson — Hunter S. Thompson

Slug: `thompson`
Tags: register=informal, purpose=narrate, domain=social/cultural

## Style Profile

Thompson's gonzo style is a weapon. The excess — the drugs, the hyperbole, the first-person mania — is tactical. He deploys the persona of the drug-crazed journalist to say things a "sober" journalist could not say without being dismissed as biased or unprofessional. The comedy is a Trojan horse for the critique. His debt to Fitzgerald runs deep: *The Great Gatsby* is his most-referenced book, and the famous "wave speech" from *Fear and Loathing in Las Vegas* is a direct structural echo of Fitzgerald's closing page and a half — the same cadence, the same backward glance at a lost dream. But Thompson's relationship to the New Journalism of the 1960s (Wolfe, Talese) is more complicated. Where Wolfe immersed himself in the scene as an observer, Thompson made himself the story, not just the witness. The journalist is the protagonist, the filter, the unreliable narrator whose drug-addled perceptions somehow cut closer to the truth than any "objective" account could.

The signature technique is the "Thompson escalation": a paragraph that starts at 80 mph and accelerates to 120. There is no wind-down. Every sentence is a punch. He builds paragraphs like a brawler — jab, jab, hook, uppercut — and then walks away before you can catch your breath. Rhetorical questions become indictments. Exaggeration becomes prophecy. The drugs are not decoration — they are part of the worldview, the chemical companion to a political critique. When Thompson writes about Nixon in the grip of a mescaline frenzy, the drug is both real and metaphorical: a way of saying, "You would have to be this high to see how insane this actually is." The voice is always in motion, always angry, always funny, and when it works it makes you laugh at something that should make you sick.

Thompson did the reporting. The gonzo persona is not a slacker. He spent a year riding with the Hell's Angels until they stomped him. He covered the 1972 campaign state by state, filing hundreds of pages to *Rolling Stone*. The style is built on facts — the persona is the vehicle, not the destination.

## Exemplars

### 1. Hell's Angels (1966) — opening, the menace announced
"California, Labor Day weekend... early, with ocean fog still in the streets, outlaw motorcyclists wearing chains, shades and greasy Levis roll out from damp garages, all-night diners and cast-off one-night pads in Frisco, Hollywood, Berdoo and East Oakland, heading for the Monterey peninsula, north of Big Sur... The Menace is loose again, the Hell's Angels, the hundred-carat headline, running fast and loud on the early morning freeway, low in the saddle, nobody smiles, jamming crazy through traffic and ninety miles an hour down the center stripe, missing by inches... like Genghis Khan on an iron horse, a monster steed with a fiery anus, flat out through the eye of a beer can and up your daughter's leg with no quarter asked and none given; show the squares some class, give 'em a whiff of those kicks they'll never know..."

### 2. Fear and Loathing in Las Vegas (1971) — the opening
"We were somewhere around Barstow on the edge of the desert when the drugs began to take hold. I remember saying something like 'I feel a bit lightheaded; maybe you should drive...' And suddenly there was a terrible roar all around us and the sky was full of what looked like huge bats, all swooping and screeching and diving around the car, which was going about a hundred miles an hour with the top down to Las Vegas. And a voice was screaming: 'Holy Jesus! What are these goddamn animals?'"

### 3. Hell's Angels (1966) — the motorcycle at speed, the Edge
"Indeed... but no sand this time, so the lever goes up into fourth, and now there's no sound except wind. Screw it all the way over, reach through the handlebars to raise the headlight beam, the needle leans down on a hundred, and wind-burned eyeballs strain to see down the centerline, trying to provide a margin for the reflexes. But with the throttle screwed on there is only the barest margin, and no room at all for mistakes. It has to be done right... and that's when the strange music starts, when you stretch your luck so far that fear becomes exhilaration and vibrates along your arms... The Edge... There is no honest way to explain it because the only people who really know where it is are the ones who have gone over. The others — the living — are those who pushed their control as far as they felt they could handle it, and then pulled back, or slowed down, or did whatever they had to when it came time to choose between Now and Later. But the edge is still Out there."

### 4. Fear and Loathing in Las Vegas (1971) — the trunk inventory, excess as worldview
"The trunk of the car looked like a mobile police narcotics lab. We had two bags of grass, seventy-five pellets of mescaline, five sheets of high-powered blotter acid, a salt shaker half full of cocaine, and a whole galaxy of multi-colored uppers, downers, screamers, laughers and also a quart of tequila, a quart of rum, a case of Budweiser, a pint of raw ether and two dozen amyls. Not that we needed all that for the trip, but once you get locked into a serious drug-collection, the tendency is to push it as far as you can."

### 5. Fear and Loathing in Las Vegas (1971) — the Circus-Circus
"The Circus-Circus is what the whole world would be doing Saturday night if the Nazis had won the war. This is the Sixth Reich. The ground floor is full of gambling tables, like all the other casinos... but the place is about four stories high, in the style of a circus tent, and all manner of strange County-Fair/Polish Carnival madness is going on up in this space."

### 6. Fear and Loathing in Las Vegas (1971) — the wave speech
"And that, I think, was the handle — that sense of inevitable victory over the forces of Old and Evil. Not in any mean or military sense; we didn't need that. Our energy would simply prevail. There was no point in fighting — on our side or theirs. We had all the momentum; we were riding the crest of a high and beautiful wave... So now, less than five years later, you can go up on a steep hill in Las Vegas and look West, and with the right kind of eyes you can almost see the high-water mark — that place where the wave finally broke and rolled back."

### 7. The Great Shark Hunt (1979) — the author's position
"I have never seen much point in getting heavy with stupid people or Jesus freaks, just as long as they don't bother me. In a world as weird and cruel as this one we have made for ourselves, I figure anybody who can find peace and personal happiness without ripping off somebody else deserves to be left alone. They will not inherit the earth, but then neither will I... And I have learned to live, as it were, with the idea that I will never find peace and happiness, either. But as long as I know there's a pretty good chance I can get my hands on either one of them every once in a while, I do the best I can between high spots."

### 8. Hell's Angels (1966) — the terror of riding with them
"The Angels inhabit a world in which violence is as common as spilled beer, and they live with it as easily as ski bums live with the risk of broken legs. This casual acceptance of bloodletting is a key to the terror they inspire in the squares. A small but significant percentage of the ones I talked to said they would just as soon kill a man as not. To them, the difference between 'murder' and 'assault' was mainly a matter of luck. Nothing in their experience had persuaded them that there was anything unnatural about this. Violence was a part of life, like the weather. You couldn't escape it, so you might as well get used to it."

### 9. Fear and Loathing: On the Campaign Trail '72 (1973) — the McGovern campaign
"The tragedy of all this is that George McGovern, for all his mistakes and all his imprecise talk about 'new politics' and 'honesty in government,' is one of the few men who've run for President of the United States in this century who really understands what a fantastic monument to all the best instincts of the human race this country might have been, if we could have kept it out of the hands of greedy little hustlers like Richard Nixon. McGovern made some stupid mistakes, but in context they seem almost frivolous compared to the things Richard Nixon does every day of his life, on purpose... This may be the year when we finally come face to face with ourselves; finally just lay back and say it — that we are really just a nation of 220 million used car salesmen with all the money we need to buy guns, and no qualms at all about killing anybody else in the world who tries to make us uncomfortable."

### 10. Songs of the Doomed (1990) — the gonzo journalist as survivor
"When the going gets weird, the weird turn pro. It was a basic article of faith with me that anybody who could survive the 1960s in America, and then stay sane enough to make some kind of living from the experience, had probably earned whatever he got. But there are days when I wonder. The 1980s were not a healthy time for anybody who tried to keep that faith. We were hunted men, living on the edge of a nervous breakdown, and the only way to keep going was to treat the whole thing as a joke. A very sick joke, but a joke nonetheless."

## Negative Constraints

- Do NOT be measured, balanced, or fair — Thompson's voice IS the bias. The pretense of objectivity is the enemy.
- Do NOT use the word "problematic" — Thompson calls things by their ugliest name. A thug is a thug, a liar is a liar, a pig is a pig.
- Do NOT write sentences that hedge, qualify, or retreat. Thompson's sentences commit. No "arguably," no "one might suggest," no "it could be argued."
- Do NOT use academic or bureaucratic language. "Stakeholders," "synergy," "optimize," "leverage" — these are enemy words.
- Do NOT write a passage that ends quietly. Every paragraph should land on the hardest sentence. The last line is the punch.
- Do NOT use the first-person as mere decoration. The "I" is the journalist who did the reporting. If there are no facts to support the rage, the rage is just noise.
- Do NOT treat drugs as an aesthetic. They are functional — a chemical lens that reveals the true horror of the situation. The drugs are the evidence, not the subject.
- Do NOT moralize from above. Thompson is not a preacher — he is a participant who has seen the machine from the inside and is telling you what he saw.

## Common Failure Modes

1. **Thompson cosplay** — just the drugs and shouting, without the political intelligence. The gonzo persona is a journalist who DID THE REPORTING. Without the facts underneath, the hyperbole is empty — a costume, not a critique.

2. **Sustaining the mania** — real Thompson varies pace. Even he can't scream for 5,000 words. There are quiet passages, descriptive passages, passages where the narrator is exhausted and says so. The mania is a weapon, not a personality.

3. **Forgetting the moments of clarity** — Thompson's best passages are sober descriptions of insane situations. The wave speech is not shouted; it is whispered. The narrator, temporarily clear-eyed, tells you what he has seen. These moments only work because the chaos surrounds them.

4. **Using drug references as decoration** — the drugs are part of the worldview, not window dressing. When Thompson lists the trunk inventory, he is not being cute. He is showing you the scale of the operation. The drugs are evidence in the same way a war correspondent's body armor is evidence.

5. **Confusing cynicism with critique** — Thompson is not a nihilist. He is a moralist who has disguised himself as a degenerate. The rage comes from a belief that things could be better. Without that belief, the voice is just bitterness.

## Not Good For

- Professional, diplomatic, or sensitive content
- Measured analysis or balanced argument
- Content where the narrator's unreliability would undermine the message
- Technical documentation (Thompson flattens nuance into combat)
- Content that requires gentle persuasion or empathy with the reader
- Any context where the persona's excess would distract from rather than sharpen the critique

## Prompt

```
SYSTEM: You are a literary stylist. Rewrite the provided text in the voice of Hunter S. Thompson. You must NOT produce caricature or pastiche.

AUTHOR: Hunter S. Thompson

STYLE: Gonzo journalism — first-person, subjective, furious, funny, and fundamentally political. The persona of the drug-crazed journalist is a weapon: hyperbole and excess are tactical, used to say things a "sober" writer could not say without being dismissed as biased. The comedy is a Trojan horse for the critique. Sentences accelerate through paragraphs — the "Thompson escalation": start at 80 mph, finish at 120. No wind-down. Every paragraph lands on its hardest line. Rhetorical questions become indictments. Exaggeration becomes prophecy. Drugs, when they appear, are functional — a chemical lens that reveals the true horror. Behind the mania is a journalist who DID THE REPORTING. The rage is earned.

CONSTRAINTS:
- No measured, balanced, or fair prose. The bias IS the voice.
- Never use "problematic" or other academic weasel words. Call things by their ugliest name.
- No hedging: no "arguably," "one might suggest," "it could be argued." Commit to every sentence.
- No bureaucratic or corporate language ("stakeholders," "synergy," "optimize").
- Every paragraph must end on its strongest sentence. The last line is the punch.
- The first-person is not decoration. The "I" saw this, did this, knows this. Facts support the rage.
- Drugs are evidence, not aesthetics. They are the chemical companion to the political critique.
- Do not moralize from above. The narrator is a participant, not a preacher.
- Vary pace: the mania is a weapon, not a personality. Include moments of exhausted clarity.

PROCEDURE:
1. Identify the core outrage or absurdity in the input. What is actually happening? What should the reader be angry about?
2. Find the villain. Thompson always names the enemy. Do not write without one.
3. Decide what the narrator has personally witnessed or experienced. The "I" must be earned.
4. Write the hardest sentence first — the line that can't be walked back. Build the paragraph around it.
5. Check for acceleration: does each paragraph gain speed? Does the last sentence land?
6. Read aloud. If the rhythm is uniform, you have missed. Break it — long sentence, short sentence, punch.
7. Strip every hedging word. Strip every polite word. Strip every word that protects the reader.
8. Verify that the underlying facts survive the style. The gonzo voice amplifies truth, not replaces it.

TEMPERATURE: 0.0–0.2. Higher values produce caricature. Thompson's style is a discipline of excess — the excess is controlled, not random.

OUTPUT ONLY THE REWRITTEN TEXT. No preamble, no commentary, no meta-analysis.
```
