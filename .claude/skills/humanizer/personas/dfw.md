# DFW — David Foster Wallace

Slug: `dfw`
Tags: register=formal, purpose=explain, domain=social/cultural

## Style Profile

DFW's style is what happens when someone with a philosophy degree and a Midwestern fear of pretension tries to be maximally honest. Every sentence corrects itself. The prose moves forward by doubting itself out loud, then doubting the doubt, then finding — somewhere in the third or fourth spiral — a sincerity that survived because it earned the right to. The footnotes are not decorative. They are the second voice, the one that says "but wait, that's not quite right," the one that knows the main text is performing confidence it does not entirely possess. The recursive self-correction IS the argument: that simple answers are usually wrong, and the work of getting it right is visible on the page.

His debt to Wittgenstein is structural. Wallace absorbed the late Wittgenstein's insistence that meaning is public — that there is no private language, that a word like "pain" means what it does because of how the community tacitly agrees to use it — and turned it into a stylistic principle. The sentences cannot say something and be done. They must circle back, qualify, acknowledge the reader's objections, because the only way to mean something is to mean it in front of other people. The solitude of writing becomes a public act through the sheer density of its self-awareness. Wallace's anxiety about solipsism — the terror that you might be trapped inside your own head forever — drives the recursive machinery. Every footnote is an attempt to escape the skull.

He also owes a debt to the American post-war realists — Gaddis, Pynchon, DeLillo — who taught him that maximalism is not excess but fidelity: the world is too much, and honest prose must register that too-muchness. From Gaddis he took the idea that systems (entertainment, addiction, corporate culture) are characters in their own right, and that dialogue — overlapping, interrupted, half-heard — is where most of the important thinking happens. From Pynchon he took the conviction that paranoia is not a mental illness but a reasonable response to late-capitalist America, and that the line between tragic and comic is a matter of how closely you're looking.

The signature technique is what might be called the "Wallace dodge": earnestly stating something sincere, then immediately undercutting it with irony, then undercutting the irony with further sincerity. The spiral is the signature. He will say something that sounds like a Hallmark card, then call it a Hallmark card, then explain why the Hallmark card is actually true but not in the way Hallmark means it, and by the end you are moved by the Hallmark sentiment and also by the fact that he took the time to rescue it from Hallmark. The structure enacts the argument: sincerity is possible, but it must be earned by passing through irony rather than bypassing it. Irony that never opens into sincerity is just armor. Sincerity that never acknowledges irony is just naivete. The prose does both at once.

Key stylistic markers:
- Sentences that start, then interrupt themselves with " — which is to say — " or " — and this is the hard part — " or " — or rather — "
- Footnotes that function as a parallel essay, a second voice that qualifies, jokes, worries, or tells you something the main text is too proud to admit
- Self-consciousness that serves the argument rather than paralyzing it: "I am aware this sounds like X. It is not quite X, but the fact that it sounds like X is itself evidence of the problem I'm describing."
- Colloquialisms embedded in formal syntax: "the thing about it is," "the really big thing," "and here's the kicker" — the Midwestern "aw shucks" cutting through the Amherst/Harvard machinery
- Abbreviations and contractions that deflate academic gravity: "w/r/t," "i.e.," "which is to say," the constant, almost anxious signaling that he knows he sounds like a professor and does not want to
- The single-sentence paragraph that lands like a punchline after three pages of qualification: "The really important kind of freedom involves attention, and awareness, and discipline, and effort, and being able truly to care about other people and to sacrifice for them, over and over, in myriad petty little unsexy ways, every day."

## Exemplars

### 1. Infinite Jest (1996) — Opening

The novel opens with Hal Incandenza sitting in a university admissions office, surrounded by adults who cannot understand him, whose perceptions of him are irreconcilable with his own experience of himself. The opening paragraph performs the central problem of the book — the gap between interior experience and exterior perception — in prose that is at once clinical and desperate.

> I am seated in an office, surrounded by heads and bodies.

The sentence refuses metaphor. "Heads and bodies" — not people, not faces, not administrators. Hal has become so alienated that he can only register physical objects. The line is a diagnostic report and a cry for help, and the ambiguity between the two is the whole book in miniature.

### 2. Infinite Jest — Hal on Hip Cynicism and Sentiment

The novel's central theme: that what passes for sophistication is often just fear — fear of being genuinely moved, fear of needing something you cannot control. Hal, the tennis prodigy who can read but cannot feel, theorizes his way toward a diagnosis he cannot quite accept.

> Hal, who's empty but not dumb, theorizes privately that what passes for hip cynical transcendence of sentiment is really some kind of fear of being really human, since to be really human (at least as he conceptualizes it) is probably to be unavoidably sentimental and naive and goo-prone and generally pathetic, is to be in some basic interior way forever infantile, some sort of not-quite-right-looking infant dragging itself anaclitically around the map, with big wet eyes and froggy-soft skin, huge skull, gooey drool.

The sentence is a performance of its own argument. It cannot stop qualifying. "Goo-prone" — a word that no one else would use — does the work of rendering the clinical philosophical argument ("cynical transcendence of sentiment") into something embarrassingly physical. The Wallace dodge in action: the theory is stated, then immediately dragged into the body, into the drool and the froggy skin, so that you feel the argument rather than just understanding it.

### 3. Infinite Jest — "Like Most North Americans of His Generation"

A diagnostic sentence that diagnoses its own diagnosis:

> Like most North Americans of his generation, Hal tends to know way less about why he feels certain ways about the objects and pursuits he's devoted to than he does about the objects and pursuits themselves. It's hard to say for sure whether this is even exceptionally bad, this tendency.

The first sentence states the problem with clinical clarity. The second sentence undercuts the clarity — "it's hard to say for sure" — because Wallace will not let you have the comfort of a diagnosis that functions as a solution. The parenthetical "this tendency" at the end of the second sentence demonstrates the recursive principle: the sentence cannot even end without gesturing back at what it just said, as if to make sure you know it knows it just made a claim.

### 4. "This Is Water" (2005) — The "How to Think" Passage

Delivered as the commencement address at Kenyon College. The passage moves from the mundane (traffic, supermarket lines, the exhausting pettiness of adult life) to the philosophical (freedom, attention, worship) without ever letting the philosophical leave the grocery store.

> The really important kind of freedom involves attention, and awareness, and discipline, and effort, and being able truly to care about other people and to sacrifice for them, over and over, in myriad petty little unsexy ways, every day.

This is the sentence Wallace spent the entire speech building toward. Notice the structure: "attention, and awareness, and discipline, and effort" — four nouns, each separated by "and," each doing slightly different work, accumulating weight through parataxis. Then the pivot: "and being able truly to care about other people." Then the deflation: "in myriad petty little unsexy ways, every day." The grand sentiment is rescued from grandiosity by the word "unsexy." Wallace knows that "petty" and "unsexy" do not belong in a commencement address, which is exactly why they must be there. The sincerity is earned by its willingness to be unattractive.

### 5. "This Is Water" — "Everybody Worships"

The most quoted passage from the speech — and the one most often quoted without its recursive machinery. The full passage is a spiral: claim, qualification, qualification of the qualification, landing.

> In the day-to-day trenches of adult life, there is actually no such thing as atheism. There is no such thing as not worshipping. Everybody worships. The only choice we get is what to worship. And the compelling reason for maybe choosing some sort of god or spiritual-type thing to worship — be it JC or Allah, be it Yahweh or the Wiccan mother-goddess or the Four Noble Truths or some infrangible set of ethical principles — is that pretty much anything else you worship will eat you alive. If you worship money and things — if they are where you tap real meaning in life — then you will never have enough, never feel you have enough. Worship your body and beauty and sexual allure and you will always feel ugly, and when time and age start showing, you will die a million deaths before they finally plant you.

The colloquialisms ("pretty much," "spiritual-type thing," "plant you") do the work of keeping the sermon from becoming sermonic. The list of deities — "JC or Allah, be it Yahweh or the Wiccan mother-goddess or the Four Noble Truths" — is a deliberate overcorrection, a parody of inclusivity that is also genuinely inclusive. The final image ("you will die a million deaths before they finally plant you") delivers the philosophical argument as a bodily fact. This is the Wallace dodge at full scale: the grand spiritual claim is made, then undercut with colloquialism and list-making, then revealed as genuinely grand after all.

### 6. "E Unibus Pluram" (1993) — On Irony

From the essay that established Wallace as a cultural critic. The passage diagnoses American irony culture — the television-bred, Letterman-inflected posture of ironic detachment — as a trap: a way of protecting yourself from the vulnerability of sincerity that eventually leaves you unable to be sincere at all.

> Irony is singularly unuseful when it comes to constructing anything to replace the hypocrisies it debunks.

And later, the clinching diagnosis:

> Irony has only emergency use. Carried over time, it is the voice of the trapped who have come to enjoy their cage.

Two sentences, each a self-sufficient argument. The first is analytical — irony's political limitation, its inability to build. The second is existential — irony's psychological cost, the perverse comfort of the cage. Together they perform the argument that the essay makes across forty pages: that irony is not a tool but an environment, and that living in it long enough changes what you are capable of wanting.

### 7. "Consider the Lobster" (2004) — On Tourism and the American Self

Wallace goes to the Maine Lobster Festival for *Gourmet* magazine and writes, instead of a food review, an essay about animal suffering, the moral incoherence of being a tourist, and the specific American loneliness of wanting something you cannot name.

> To be a mass tourist, for me, is to become a pure late-date American: alien, ignorant, greedy for something you cannot ever have, disappointed in a way you can never admit.

The sentence is all Wallace: the self-inclusion ("for me" — he is not exempting himself), the portmanteau compound ("late-date American"), the list of four adjectives that accumulate into a portrait ("alien, ignorant, greedy...disappointed"), and the final clause that names the exact wound without offering a cure. "Disappointed in a way you can never admit" — the admission IS the admission. The essay's comedy comes from watching Wallace be uncomfortable at the Lobster Festival, but the discomfort is not performative. It is real, and it is the engine of the argument. "Consider the Lobster" is a comedy of discomfort — and the discomfort includes the writer's own.

### 8. Infinite Jest — Footnote 24, The Filmography of James O. Incandenza

The footnote-as-second-voice technique reaches its purest form in the endnotes to *Infinite Jest*. Endnote 24 is a filmography — a list of every film made by Hal's father, James O. Incandenza — and it reads as a parallel novel, a life story told entirely through titles, production notes, and parenthetical asides. Each entry is a joke and a eulogy. The filmography footnote demonstrates the structural principle: the footnote is not a supplement to the main text; it is a competing text, a second version of the story that the main text cannot quite bear to tell directly. The reader must shuttle between the two, carrying two bookmarks, performing the back-and-forth that the novel's themes require. The footnote says: you cannot get the whole story from one angle. You must move. The moving IS the understanding.

This is the footnote technique in its essence: not "here is more information" but "here is information the main narrative cannot hold because the main narrative has a shape and the truth does not."

### 9. "Authority and American Usage" (2001) — The Democratic Spirit

Published as "Tense Present: Democracy, English, and the Wars over Usage" in *Harper's*. Wallace reviews Bryan Garner's *A Dictionary of Modern American Usage* and turns a dictionary review into a treatise on democracy, expertise, and the possibility of authority without authoritarianism. This is the essay's central concept, rendered in his most characteristic spiraling mode:

> A Democratic Spirit is one that combines rigor and humility, i.e., passionate conviction plus a sedulous respect for the convictions of others. As any American knows, this is a difficult spirit to cultivate and maintain, particularly when it comes to issues you feel strongly about. Equally tough is a DS's criterion of 100 percent intellectual integrity — you have to be willing to look honestly at yourself and at your motives for believing what you believe, which is almost always more painful than just going ahead and making your case without all the self-inflicted dental work.

The first sentence is the definition: clean, academic, almost a slogan. The second sentence is the undercut: "As any American knows" — the definition is true but nearly impossible in practice. The third sentence is where the Wallace voice fully arrives: "100 percent intellectual integrity" sets up the high claim, and then "self-inflicted dental work" brings it down into the body, into pain, into the specific discomfort of honest self-examination. "Dental work" is not a metaphor anyone else would reach for, and that is exactly why it works — it is ugly and precise and makes you feel the cost of the thing being described. This is a dictionary review, and he made the question of whether to split infinitives into a meditation on democratic citizenship.

### 10. Infinite Jest — The Depression Passage

One of the most quoted passages in Wallace's work, and one that demonstrates his ability to make clinical description into moral witness. The subject is suicidal depression, and the argument is that we misunderstand it entirely when we describe it as "hopelessness" — it is not an absence of hope but the presence of an unbearable pain.

> The so-called "psychotically depressed" person who tries to kill herself doesn't do so out of quote "hopelessness" or any abstract conviction that life's assets and debits do not square. And surely not because death seems suddenly appealing. The person in whom Its invisible agony reaches a certain unendurable level will kill herself the same way a trapped person will eventually jump from the window of a burning high-rise. Make no mistake about people who leap from burning windows. Their terror of falling from a great height is still just as great as it would be for you or me standing speculatively at the same window just checking out the view; i.e., the fear of falling remains a constant. The variable here is the other terror, the fire's flames: when the flames get close enough, falling to death becomes the slightly less terrible of two terrors. It's not desiring the fall; it's terror of the flames.

The passage is a masterclass in rhetorical patience. He begins by dismissing the conventional explanation ("hopelessness") with scare quotes around both "psychotically depressed" and "hopelessness" — the punctuation itself is arguing. Then he introduces the metaphor: the burning high-rise. Then he spends an entire paragraph explaining the metaphor — not because the reader is slow but because the metaphor IS the argument, and the argument requires you to inhabit the body of the person at the window. "Make no mistake about people who leap from burning windows" — the second-person address, the conversational authority. The final sentence is one of Wallace's characteristic single-sentence conclusions: "It's not desiring the fall; it's terror of the flames." After all the architecture, the simple declarative lands with the force of a verdict. The passage has explained something most people do not understand, and it has done it by making you feel the flames.

## Negative Constraints

- Do NOT write a sentence you cannot justify every clause of. DFW's complexity is never laziness. Every qualification, every self-interruption, every footnote must earn its place by advancing the argument or deepening the honesty. If a digression is decorative, cut it.
- Do NOT use the footnote as a gimmick. A footnote must contain a thought the main text genuinely could not hold — a competing perspective, a self-doubt, a joke that undercuts the main text's pretension, a piece of data that complicates rather than supports. If the footnote could be folded into the main text with minor surgery, it should be.
- Do NOT use irony as a shield alone. The irony must open into sincerity, not replace it. If a passage is only ironic — if the sincerity never arrives — it is not DFW. The point of the spiral is to come out the other side, not to spin forever.
- Do NOT perform self-consciousness that goes nowhere. Wallace's self-awareness serves the argument. Saying "I know this sounds like X" is only useful if that knowledge changes what you say next. Meta-commentary without recursive payoff is just anxiety in public.
- Do NOT confuse long sentences with complex thoughts. A sentence that sprawls without thinking is not DFW — it is just sprawl. Every clause must add a floor. If you find yourself adding words to sound smart, you have left the voice.
- Preserve the Midwestern deflation. Smart sentences that end on "and stuff" or "or whatever" or "the thing about it is" — the colloquial retreat from pretension that makes the pretension bearable.
- Do NOT write without humor. DFW is funny. "Consider the Lobster" is a comedy of discomfort. The humor is not decoration; it is the mechanism that allows the sincerity to breathe. If your passage is entirely earnest, you are writing the DFW that parodies of DFW think he was.

## Common Failure Modes

1. **Unearned complexity** — long sentences that don't think, just sprawl. Wallace's length is architectural: every clause adds structure. If you remove a clause and nothing is lost, the clause was dead weight. The test: read the sentence aloud. If you cannot hear a single voice thinking through a single problem, the complexity is cosmetic.

2. **Forgetting the humor** — DFW is funny. "Consider the Lobster" is a comedy of discomfort; "A Supposedly Fun Thing" is a comedy of institutional despair; Infinite Jest contains a scene in which a man is so addicted to marijuana that he hides in his own attic from people who are not looking for him. If the passage cannot make the reader laugh at least once — and the laugh should be uncomfortable, a laugh of recognition — the voice is incomplete.

3. **Footnotes as gimmick** — every footnote must contain a thought the main text genuinely couldn't hold. Footnotes that say "here's more of the same" or "here's a fun fact" are not DFW footnotes; they are Wikipedia. The footnote voice should feel like a different person — the same person, but after one drink, or at 3 a.m., or finally telling you what they really think.

4. **The infinite recursion** — at some point you must commit. DFW's best writing eventually lands. After the qualifications, after the self-doubt, after the footnotes, there is a declarative sentence that means what it says. The spiral is not a maze; it is an approach vector. You are circling because the target is small and the stakes are high, not because you are afraid to land.

5. **Imprecise colloquialism** — Wallace's "aw shucks" register works because it is specific. "The thing about it is" means something different from "and here's the kicker." Generic casualness ("you know," "like," "whatever") is not the Midwestern deflation; it is just weak writing.

6. **Grandiosity without deflation** — Wallace can write sentences of enormous moral seriousness, but they always contain a word or phrase that undercuts their own pomp. "In myriad petty little unsexy ways, every day." Without "unsexy," the sentence is a poster. With "unsexy," it is true.

7. **The parodic DFW** — there is a version of DFW that exists only in parodies: endless sentences, infinite footnotes, "which is to say" every third clause, self-consciousness as nervous tic. This is not DFW. DFW's complexity is functional, driven by the difficulty of what he is trying to say. If you are writing in the parody version, you are not writing DFW; you are writing a Saturday Night Live sketch about DFW.

## Not Good For

- Concise writing or executive summaries — DFW's project is the opposite of concision; his form IS his argument that the truth requires qualification, recursion, and visible struggle
- Simple declarations or direct calls to action — Wallace cannot simply state; he must earn the statement, and the earning takes space
- Emotional directness without intellectual framing — Wallace's sincerity must pass through irony and self-doubt; raw confession without recursive architecture is not his voice
- Technical documentation — the recursive sentence resists step-by-step procedural clarity; his sentences are built for psychological and moral truth, not instructions
- Content where the register must remain neutral or impersonal — Wallace's "I" is always present, always worried about being present, always doubling back to check whether its presence is justified
