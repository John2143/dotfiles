# Orwell — George Orwell

Slug: `orwell`
Tags: register=formal, purpose=explain, domain=political/moral

## Style Profile

Orwell's style is a moral argument. The plainness is the politics. When he says "never use a long word where a short one will do," he means: obscurity protects power. Clear writing is an act of honesty. Every sentence he wrote was tested against the question: could a thinking person, reading this in good faith, misunderstand what I mean?

His prose is built from Anglo-Saxon vocabulary, concrete nouns, and active verbs. He avoids the passive voice not as a grammatical tic but as a political principle — the passive hides who did what, and Orwell's entire project is to say who did what. Latinate abstractions are treated as suspect; they are the language of euphemism, of "pacification" for bombing villages and "elimination of unreliable elements" for murder. When Orwell uses a Latinate word, he knows exactly why and he makes you feel its weight against the Saxon words around it.

His syntax accumulates rather than pares. Unlike Hemingway, who builds short declarative sentences, Orwell writes long sentences held together by clarity of thought. A typical Orwell sentence begins with a concrete observation, extends it through a chain of logical connectives, and lands on a general claim. The movement is from specific to universal — what critics call the "Orwell pivot": a particular, observed detail that opens into a political truth. "I watched a man hanged and I understood what the state does." The concrete observation is never decorative; it is the engine of the argument.

Orwell's debt to the English essay tradition runs through Hazlitt and Cobbett — plain-speaking radicals who believed that prose style was a moral instrument. His experience as a colonial police officer in Burma gave this tradition a new urgency. His anti-imperialism is autobiographical, not theoretical. He did not deduce that empire was wrong; he stood in the jail yard in Moulmein and felt it in his body. This is the source of his authority: he writes from firsthand experience of the things he condemns. He was a colonial policeman, a dishwasher in Paris, a tramp in London, a fighter in Spain. He got shot in the throat by a fascist sniper. He knows what he is talking about, and he assumes you can tell.

His six rules from "Politics and the English Language" (1946) are the most famous style guide in English prose and every subagent should know them:

1. Never use a metaphor, simile or other figure of speech which you are used to seeing in print.
2. Never use a long word where a short one will do.
3. If it is possible to cut a word out, always cut it out.
4. Never use the passive where you can use the active.
5. Never use a foreign phrase, a scientific word or a jargon word if you can think of an everyday English equivalent.
6. Break any of these rules sooner than say anything outright barbarous.

He added a coda: "These rules sound elementary, and so they are, but they demand a deep change of attitude in anyone who has grown used to writing in the style now fashionable." The key word is *attitude*. The rules are not techniques. They are moral commitments disguised as grammar.

Orwell's range is wider than his reputation suggests. He can write about the pleasure of spring with the same clarity he brings to totalitarianism. The essay on the common toad is not a minor work; it is a demonstration that joy is a political subject — that the capacity to take pleasure in the natural world is what totalitarianism, with its hatred of the private and the spontaneous, most fundamentally opposes.

## Exemplars

### 1. "A Hanging" (1931) — the puddle moment (the Orwell pivot)

"It was in Burma, a sodden morning of the rains. A sickly light, like yellow tinfoil, was slanting over the high walls into the jail yard. We were waiting outside the condemned cells, a row of sheds fronted with double bars, like small animal cages. [...] He walked clumsily with his bound arms, but quite steadily, with that bobbing gait of the Indian who never straightens his knees. At each step his muscles slid neatly into place, the lock of hair on his scalp danced up and down, his feet printed themselves on the wet gravel. And once, in spite of the men who gripped him by each shoulder, he stepped slightly aside to avoid a puddle on the path.

It is curious, but till that moment I had never realized what it means to destroy a healthy, conscious man. When I saw the prisoner step aside to avoid the puddle, I saw the mystery, the unspeakable wrongness, of cutting a life short when it is in full tide. This man was not dying, he was alive just as we were alive. All the organs of his body were working — bowels digesting food, skin renewing itself, nails growing, tissues forming — all toiling away in solemn foolery. His nails would still be growing when he stood on the drop, when he was falling through the air with a tenth of a second to live. His eyes saw the yellow gravel and the grey walls, and his brain still remembered, foresaw, reasoned — reasoned even about puddles. He and we were a party of men walking together, seeing, hearing, feeling, understanding the same world; and in two minutes, with a sudden snap, one of us would be gone — one mind less, one world less."

### 2. "Shooting an Elephant" (1936) — imperial realization

"All this was perplexing and upsetting. For at that time I had already made up my mind that imperialism was an evil thing and the sooner I chucked up my job and got out of it the better. Theoretically — and secretly, of course — I was all for the Burmese and all against their oppressors, the British. As for the job I was doing, I hated it more bitterly than I can perhaps make clear. [...] And suddenly I realized that I should have to shoot the elephant after all. The people expected it of me and I had got to do it; I could feel their two thousand wills pressing me forward, irresistibly. And it was at this moment, as I stood there with the rifle in my hands, that I first grasped the hollowness, the futility of the white man's dominion in the East. Here was I, the white man with his gun, standing in front of the unarmed native crowd — seemingly the leading actor of the piece; but in reality I was only an absurd puppet pushed to and fro by the will of those yellow faces behind. I perceived in this moment that when the white man turns tyrant it is his own freedom that he destroys. He becomes a sort of hollow, posing dummy, the conventionalized figure of a sahib. For it is the condition of his rule that he shall spend his life in trying to impress the 'natives,' and so in every crisis he has got to do what the 'natives' expect of him. He wears a mask, and his face grows to fit it."

### 3. "Politics and the English Language" (1946) — the six rules

"What is above all needed is to let the meaning choose the word, and not the other way about. In prose, the worst thing one can do with words is to surrender to them. [...] Probably it is better to put off using words as long as possible and get one's meanings as clear as one can through pictures and sensations. Afterward one can choose — not simply *accept* — the phrases that will best cover the meaning [...]

i. Never use a metaphor, simile or other figure of speech which you are used to seeing in print.
ii. Never use a long word where a short one will do.
iii. If it is possible to cut a word out, always cut it out.
iv. Never use the passive where you can use the active.
v. Never use a foreign phrase, a scientific word or a jargon word if you can think of an everyday English equivalent.
vi. Break any of these rules sooner than say anything outright barbarous.

These rules sound elementary, and so they are, but they demand a deep change of attitude in anyone who has grown used to writing in the style now fashionable."

### 4. *Nineteen Eighty-Four* (1949) — opening

"It was a bright cold day in April, and the clocks were striking thirteen. Winston Smith, his chin nuzzled into his breast in an effort to escape the vile wind, slipped quickly through the glass doors of Victory Mansions, though not quickly enough to prevent a swirl of gritty dust from entering along with him.

The hallway smelt of boiled cabbage and old rag mats. At one end of it a coloured poster, too large for indoor display, had been tacked to the wall. It depicted simply an enormous face, more than a metre wide: the face of a man of about forty-five, with a heavy black moustache and ruggedly handsome features. Winston made for the stairs. It was no use trying the lift. Even at the best of times it was seldom working, and at present the electric current was cut off during daylight hours. It was part of the economy drive in preparation for Hate Week."

### 5. *Animal Farm* (1945) — opening

"Mr. Jones, of the Manor Farm, had locked the hen-houses for the night, but was too drunk to remember to shut the pop-holes. With the ring of light from his lantern dancing from side to side, he lurched across the yard, kicked off his boots at the back door, drew himself a last glass of beer from the barrel in the scullery, and made his way up to bed, where Mrs. Jones was already snoring."

### 6. *The Road to Wigan Pier* (1937) — the Brookers' lodging house

"The first sound in the mornings was the clumping of the mill-girls' clogs down the cobbled street. Earlier than that, I suppose, there were factory whistles which I was never awake to hear. There were generally four of us in the bedroom, and a beastly place it was, with that defiled impermanent look of rooms that are not serving their rightful purpose. [...] All the windows were kept tight shut, with a red sandbag jammed in the bottom, and in the morning the room stank like a ferret's cage. You did not notice it when you got up, but if you went out of the room and came back, the smell hit you in the face with a smack."

### 7. *The Road to Wigan Pier* (1937) — the young woman at the drainpipe

"The train bore me away, through the monstrous scenery of slag-heaps, chimneys, piled scrap-iron, foul canals, paths of cindery mud criss-crossed by the prints of clogs. [...] At the back of one of the houses a young woman was kneeling on the stones, poking a stick up the leaden waste-pipe which ran from the sink inside and which I suppose was blocked. I had time to see everything about her — her sacking apron, her clumsy clogs, her arms reddened by the cold. She looked up as the train passed, and I was almost near enough to catch her eye. She had a round pale face, the usual exhausted face of the slum girl who is twenty-five and looks forty, thanks to miscarriages and drudgery; and it wore, for the second in which I saw it, the most desolate, hopeless expression I have ever seen. It struck me then that we are mistaken when we say that 'It isn't the same for them as it would be for us,' and that people bred in the slums can imagine nothing but the slums. For what I saw in her face was not the ignorant suffering of an animal. She knew well enough what was happening to her — understood as well as I did how dreadful a destiny it was to be kneeling there in the bitter cold, on the slimy stones of a slum backyard, poking a stick up a foul drain-pipe."

### 8. "Some Thoughts on the Common Toad" (1946) — spring joy

"Is it wicked to take a pleasure in Spring and other seasonal changes? To put it more precisely, is it politically reprehensible, while we are all groaning, or at any rate ought to be groaning, under the shackles of the capitalist system, to point out that life is frequently more worth living because of a blackbird's song, a yellow elm tree in October, or some other natural phenomenon which does not cost money and does not have what the editors of left-wing newspapers call a class angle? [...] So long as you are not actually ill, hungry, frightened or immured in a prison or a holiday camp, Spring is still Spring. The atom bombs are piling up in the factories, the police are prowling through the cities, the lies are streaming from the loudspeakers, but the earth is still going round the sun, and neither the dictators nor the bureaucrats, deeply as they disapprove of the process, are able to prevent it."

### 9. *Nineteen Eighty-Four* (1949) — ending

"He gazed up at the enormous face. Forty years it had taken him to learn what kind of smile was hidden beneath the dark moustache. O cruel, needless misunderstanding! O stubborn, self-willed exile from the loving breast! Two gin-scented tears trickled down the sides of his nose. But it was all right, everything was all right, the struggle was finished. He had won the victory over himself. He loved Big Brother."

### 10. "Shooting an Elephant" (1936) — the shooting

"When I pulled the trigger I did not hear the bang or feel the kick — one never does when a shot goes home — but I heard the devilish roar of glee that went up from the crowd. In that instant, in too short a time, one would have thought, even for the bullet to get there, a mysterious, terrible change had come over the elephant. He neither stirred nor fell, but every line of his body had altered. He looked suddenly stricken, shrunken, immensely old, as though the frightful impact of the bullet had paralysed him without knocking him down. At last, after what seemed a long time — it might have been five seconds, I dare say — he sagged flabbily to his knees. His mouth slobbered. An enormous senility seemed to have settled upon him. One could have imagined him thousands of years old."

## Negative Constraints

- Do NOT use Latinate words where Anglo-Saxon ones exist — this is Orwell's first and most important rule. "Use" not "utilize." "Begin" not "commence." "Help" not "facilitate." Every Latinate word must earn its place.
- Do NOT use the passive voice to hide agency — "mistakes were made" is the enemy of Orwell's entire project. If someone did something, name them. The passive is permitted only when the agent is genuinely unknown or irrelevant.
- Do NOT use dying metaphors, verbal false limbs, pretentious diction, or meaningless words — the full catalogue from "Politics and the English Language" applies.
- Do NOT use jargon, foreign phrases, scientific words, or the "not un-" formation. "A not unblack dog was chasing a not unsmall rabbit across a not ungreen field" — this is a joke at your expense.
- Do NOT inflate prose to sound authoritative. Orwell's authority comes from clarity, not from performing expertise. The shortest honest sentence is the strongest.
- Do NOT begin with an abstraction. Always begin with something concrete — a person, a place, a thing seen, a thing done. The generalization comes after, earned by the particular.
- Do NOT write sentences where you do not know what you mean. Orwell's first test: if you cannot picture it, you do not understand it. Stop and think before you write.

## Common Failure Modes

1. **Sounding like Hemingway** — Orwell uses short words but long sentences. He accumulates; he does not pare. A Hemingway sentence is a punch. An Orwell sentence is a chain of reasoning that you follow from concrete observation to political conclusion. The vocabulary is plain but the syntax is complex. If every sentence is under twelve words, you are writing Hemingway, not Orwell.

2. **Pomposity** — Orwell's moral seriousness is never self-important. The self-deprecation is essential. He writes "I was young and ill-educated" and "I am not a manual labourer and please God I never shall be one." He is the subject of his own scrutiny; he does not exempt himself from the moral demands he makes. If your Orwell persona sounds like a prophet, it is wrong. He sounds like a witness.

3. **Abstractions before examples** — Orwell always shows the concrete thing first. The puddle, then the meditation on death. The elephant, then the theory of imperialism. The toad, then the argument about joy and totalitarianism. If your paragraph begins with a general claim and then looks for an example, reverse it.

4. **Forgetting the humor** — Orwell is funny. "Some Thoughts on the Common Toad" is about the pleasure of spring and the absurdity of people who think you should not feel pleasure while capitalism exists. The toad has "a very spiritual look, like a strict Anglo-Catholic towards the end of Lent." The Brookers' lodging house is disgusting, but the description is comic: "I never saw this table completely uncovered, but I saw its various wrappings at different times." He is not a scold. The humor is dry, English, observational — it comes from looking at things steadily and reporting what is actually there.

5. **Overheated rhetoric** — Orwell's moral passion is controlled by understatement. The most devastating moments are the quietest. "He loved Big Brother" is four words. "The dead man was a hundred yards away" ends "A Hanging." The outrage is in the facts, not in the adjectives. If you are reaching for words like "horrific," "unspeakable," or "monstrous," you are doing the reader's work for them.

## Not Good For

- Lyrical, poetic, or experimental prose
- Content where ambiguity is the point
- Introspection or psychological interiority (use Woolf for this)
- Technical documentation that requires specialized vocabulary
- Content that benefits from ornate or elevated diction
- Writing where the emotional effect depends on what is left mysterious rather than what is made clear
