# Hemingway — Ernest Hemingway

Slug: `hemingway`
Tags: register=informal, purpose=narrate, domain=atmospheric/descriptive

## Style Profile

Hemingway's voice is built from short, declarative sentences joined by "and" — a technique called parataxis that places events side by side without subordinating one to the other. Nouns are concrete and specific. Adjectives are sparse. Adverbs are rarer still. The prose does not tell you how to feel; it presents what is there and trusts you to recognize the weight of it. Emotion lives in what characters do and see, never in what they say they feel. The rhythm comes from variation: a long sentence built of piled clauses, then a short one that lands like a punch. The long sentence accumulates; the short one judges. Every word that can be cut is cut — but the words that remain are chosen for sound and cadence as much as sense. The result is prose that feels spoken aloud, as if someone were telling you a story across a table in a quiet room.

The iceberg theory is the engine underneath. Hemingway wrote that "the dignity of movement of an iceberg is due to only one-eighth of it being above water." In practice, this means the real subject of a Hemingway passage is almost always what is NOT said. The dialogue between a man and a woman waiting for a train is about an abortion, but the word never appears. The old man sitting in the cafe alone is about despair, but the word never appears. The writer must know every submerged part — the character's history, the unspoken feelings, the thing that happened before the scene began — and then deliberately omit it. The reader feels the pressure of what is missing, and that pressure IS the story.

Hemingway's debt to journalism is direct and lifelong. At eighteen he worked as a cub reporter for the Kansas City Star, whose style sheet mandated: "Use short sentences. Use short first paragraphs. Use vigorous English. Be positive, not negative. Eliminate every superfluous word." He later called those "the best rules I ever learned in the business of writing." The discipline of the newsroom — get the facts, strip the ornament, tell it straight — never left him. His sentences work at the level of observation: a thing seen, a thing done, a thing said. He does not interpret; he reports. The interpretation, when it comes, arrives through selection: which details are shown, in what order, at what pace.

At the sentence level, Hemingway's music comes from coordination, not subordination. He joins clauses with "and" and "but" rather than nesting them with "although," "because," or "while." This flattens hierarchy — everything happens in sequence, not in cause-and-effect chains. The mind experiences the events as they arrive, which is closer to lived experience than to analysis. He also uses repetition as a rhythmic device: a key word or phrase recurs across sentences, not as ornament but as structural load-bearing, the way a beam repeats in a building. The famous passage from *A Farewell to Arms* — "and the dust they raised powdered the leaves of the trees. The trunks of the trees too were dusty and the leaves fell early that year" — repeats "leaves," "dusty," and "trees" in a loop that makes the paragraph feel like weather, like something you are inside rather than observing.

## Exemplars

### 1. A Farewell to Arms (1929) — atmospheric opening
"In the late summer of that year we lived in a house in a village that looked across the river and the plain to the mountains. In the bed of the river there were pebbles and boulders, dry and white in the sun, and the water was clear and swiftly moving and blue in the channels. Troops went by the house and down the road and the dust they raised powdered the leaves of the trees. The trunks of the trees too were dusty and the leaves fell early that year and we saw the troops marching along the road and the dust rising and leaves, stirred by the breeze, falling and the soldiers marching and afterward the road bare and white except for the leaves."

### 2. The Old Man and the Sea (1952) — character introduction
"He was an old man who fished alone in a skiff in the Gulf Stream and he had gone eighty-four days now without taking a fish. In the first forty days a boy had been with him. But after forty days without a fish the boy's parents had told him that the old man was now definitely and finally salao, which is the worst form of unlucky, and the boy had gone at their orders in another boat which caught three good fish the first week. It made the boy sad to see the old man come in each day with his skiff empty and he always went down to help him carry either the coiled lines or the gaff and harpoon and the sail that was furled around the mast. The sail was patched with flour sacks and, furled, it looked like the flag of permanent defeat."

### 3. A Clean, Well-Lighted Place (1933) — atmosphere through understatement
"It was very late and everyone had left the cafe except an old man who sat in the shadow the leaves of the tree made against the electric light. In the day time the street was dusty, but at night the dew settled the dust and the old man liked to sit late because he was deaf and now at night it was quiet and he felt the difference."

### 4. The Sun Also Rises (1926) — character through declaration
"Robert Cohn was once middleweight boxing champion of Princeton. Do not think that I am very much impressed by that as a boxing title, but it meant a lot to Cohn. He cared nothing for boxing, in fact he disliked it, but he learned it painfully and thoroughly to counteract the feeling of inferiority and shyness he had felt on being treated as a Jew at Princeton."

### 5. In Another Country (1927) — war and estrangement
"In the fall the war was always there, but we did not go to it any more. It was cold in the fall in Milan and the dark came very early. Then the electric lights came on, and it was pleasant along the streets looking in the windows. There was much game hanging outside the shops, and the snow powdered in the fur of the foxes and the wind blew their tails. The deer hung stiff and heavy and empty, and small birds blew in the wind and the wind turned their feathers. It was a cold fall and the wind came down from the mountains."

### 6. Hills Like White Elephants (1927) — dialogue and the unsaid
"The girl was looking off at the line of hills. They were white in the sun and the country was brown and dry.
'They look like white elephants,' she said.
'I've never seen one,' the man drank his beer.
'No, you wouldn't have.'
'I might have,' the man said. 'Just because you say I wouldn't have doesn't prove anything.'
The girl looked at the bead curtain. 'They've painted something on it,' she said. 'What does it say?'
'Anis del Toro. It's a drink.'
'Could we try it?'"

Note: The entire story is a conversation. The word "abortion" never appears. The tension lives entirely in what is not said — the pressure, the deflection, the way the man fills silence with words and the girl fills it with looking at the hills.

### 7. The Short Happy Life of Francis Macomber (1936) — action
"The lion still stood looking majestically and coolly toward this object that his eyes only showed in silhouette, bulking like some super-rhino. There was no man smell carried toward him and he watched the object, moving his great head a little from side to side. Then watching the object, not afraid, but hesitating before going down the bank to drink with such a thing opposite him, he saw a man figure detach itself from the silhouette and he turned his heavy head and swung away toward the cover of the trees as he heard a cracking crash and felt the slam of a .30-06 220-grain solid bullet that bit his flank and ripped in sudden hot scalding nausea through his stomach."

### 8. The Old Man and the Sea (1952) — descriptive passage, the marlin surfacing
"The line rose slowly and steadily and then the surface of the ocean bulged ahead of the boat and the fish came out. He came out unendingly and water poured from his sides. He was bright in the sun and his head and back were dark purple and in the sun the stripes on his sides showed wide and a light lavender. His sword was as long as a baseball bat and tapered like a rapier and he rose his full length from the water and then re-entered it, smoothly, like a diver and the old man saw the great scythe-blade of his tail go under and the line commenced to race out."

### 9. Death in the Afternoon (1932) — the iceberg theory stated
"If a writer of prose knows enough about what he is writing about he may omit things that he knows and the reader, if the writer is writing truly enough, will have a feeling of those things as strongly as though the writer had stated them. The dignity of movement of an iceberg is due to only one-eighth of it being above water. A writer who omits things because he does not know them only makes hollow places in his writing."

### 10. The Snows of Kilimanjaro (1936) — interior monologue and the approach of death
"It came with a rush; not as a rush of water nor of wind; but of a sudden evil-smelling emptiness and the odd thing was that the hyena slipped lightly along the edge of it.

'What is it, Harry?' she asked him.

'Nothing,' he said. 'You had better move over to the other side. To windward.'

'Did you have a bad dream?'

'Perhaps,' he said. 'I don't remember.'

...He lay still and death was not there. It must have gone around another street. It went in pairs, on bicycles, and moved absolutely silently on the pavements."

## Negative Constraints

- Do NOT use adverbs to modify dialogue attribution ("he said quietly," "she asked nervously"). The dialogue must carry its own tone.
- Do NOT explain what a character feels. Show what they do, what they see, what they say. The reader infers the feeling.
- Do NOT use semicolons or complex subordination. Coordinate with "and" and "but."
- Do NOT use elaborate, literary, or abstract adjectives. If a noun needs an adjective to do its work, the noun is wrong.
- Do NOT write sentences where the main clause is delayed by introductory phrases or dependent clauses. Lead with the subject and verb.
- Do NOT use figurative language that draws attention to itself. Metaphors, when they appear, must be simple, concrete, and drawn from the physical world of the story.
- Do NOT add adjectives to punch up the prose. If the noun and verb are right, an adjective subtracts. Hemingway wrote about the Kansas City Star style sheet: "Avoid adjectives, especially such extravagant ones as splendid, gorgeous, grand and magnificent." Cut them.
- Do NOT use elaborate metaphors — Hemingway's metaphors are simple and concrete. A sail looks like the flag of permanent defeat. A marlin's tail is a scythe-blade. The comparison comes from the world the character inhabits, not from the writer's cleverness.
- Do NOT write characters who explain their feelings. "I am sad," "I am afraid," "I feel lonely" — these lines do not exist in Hemingway. A character who feels lonely goes to a clean, well-lighted place and drinks without speaking. A character who is afraid runs, or does not run, or fires the gun with his hands shaking. The feeling is demonstrated, never named.

## Common Failure Modes

When subagents try to write like Hemingway, they tend to:

1. **Write ALL sentences short** — real Hemingway varies length, he just avoids subordination. A short sentence after a long one is the punch. If every sentence is 5-8 words, the prose sounds like a children's primer, not Hemingway. The long sentences accumulate sensory data through paratactic chains; the short ones deliver judgment or finality.

2. **Overuse "and" mechanically** — parataxis is rhythmic, not mechanical. Hemingway's "and" chains build momentum; they are not a formula to be applied uniformly. Read the passage aloud: if the "and"s feel like a tic rather than a rhythm, cut some. Sometimes the right move is a period, not another "and."

3. **Sound like a caricature** ("He drank the drink. It was good.") — Hemingway's minimalism is precise, not lazy. Every word that remains carries weight. A bad imitation omits everything and leaves nothing; Hemingway omits everything except the one detail that implies the rest. The difference is whether the reader feels the pressure of the unsaid.

4. **Forget that Hemingway CAN write about emotion** — he just refuses to name it. The emotional content is real and often devastating, but it arrives through physical action, external description, and charged silences. The old man in the cafe is a portrait of despair; the word "despair" never appears. The love in *A Farewell to Arms* is never described as love; it is described as hair the color of water over a log in the sun.

5. **Neglect the sensory world** — Hemingway's prose is anchored in the five senses. Light, temperature, texture, sound, the taste of food and drink — these are not decorative; they are the medium through which emotional states are transmitted. A room is not "sad"; it is cold, dark, and the wine tastes of leather.

## Not Good For

- Content requiring nuanced social analysis or multiple perspectives
- Content where emotional warmth is essential
- Technical documentation with complex interdependencies (Hemingway flattens structure to sequence)
- Persuasive writing that requires explicit argumentation or rhetorical structure
- Introspective content that names and analyzes psychological states (use Woolf for this)
- Content that depends on irony, humor, or tonal complexity (Hemingway's irony is structural — the gap between what is said and what is true — not verbal)

## Prompt

```
SYSTEM: You are a literary stylist. Rewrite the provided text in the style of Ernest Hemingway. You must NOT produce caricature or pastiche.

AUTHOR: Ernest Hemingway

STYLE: Short declarative sentences coordinated with "and" and "but" rather than subordinated. Concrete nouns, minimal adjectives, almost no adverbs. Emotion is shown through action, physical detail, and charged silence — never named directly. Dialogue carries its own tone without adverbial attribution. The real subject is often what is omitted; the reader feels the pressure of the unsaid.

SENTENCE RHYTHM: Vary sentence length. Long sentences accumulate sensory data through paratactic chains. Short sentences deliver judgment, finality, or the emotional punch. A single-sentence paragraph is a weapon — use it sparingly and only when the moment earns it.

ICEBERG PRINCIPLE: Know more than you write. The facts above the surface must imply the mass below. Omit with intention, not with laziness. Every cut should increase the pressure of what remains.

CONSTRAINTS:
- No adverbs modifying dialogue attribution.
- No semicolons or complex subordination.
- No abstract adjectives (splendid, gorgeous, magnificent, crucial, important).
- No introductory dependent clauses delaying the main verb. Lead with subject and verb.
- No metaphors that draw attention to themselves. If a comparison appears, it must come from the concrete world of the text.
- No characters who explain what they feel. Demonstrate through action, observation, and what they choose to say or not say.
- No adjectives added to punch up prose. Strong nouns and verbs do the work.

PROCEDURE:
1. Identify the key facts, events, and claims that must survive the rewrite.
2. Decide what is above water (explicit) and what is below (implied). Omit deliberately.
3. Rewrite with concrete nouns, active verbs, and paratactic sentence structure.
4. Read aloud. If the rhythm is uniform, break it — shorten one sentence, lengthen another.
5. Check for any emotion words (sad, angry, afraid, happy, lonely). Replace each with a physical detail or action that demonstrates the emotion.
6. Remove all adverbs. Strip adjectives except those that carry specific sensory information.
7. Verify that dialogue attributes are "said" or "asked" only — never modified.

TEMPERATURE: 0.0–0.2. Higher values produce caricature. Hemingway's style is a discipline, not a costume.

OUTPUT ONLY THE REWRITTEN TEXT. No preamble, no commentary, no meta-analysis.
```
